History

Donald Chamberlini and Raymond Boyce began work on the relational database language after learning about the relational model from E. Codd, meeting with him at a symposium in New York in 1972. According to Chamberlin's memoirs, it was a "revelation." The authors were impressed by the expressiveness and compactness of relational algebra and relational calculus proposed by E. Codd to represent complex queries. Codd used symbolic notation with mathematical notation of operations, but Chamberlin and Boyce wanted to design the language so that it could be used by any user, even without programming skills and knowledge of mathematics. Pronunciation

Despite the fact that the abbreviation SQL should formally be pronounced as "es-q-el", the original version of the abbreviated name of the language — SEQUEL — coincided in spelling with the word sequel and is still often pronounced as "sequel". Differences in pronunciation are observed even among specialists. So, one of the authors of the language, D. Chamberlin uses the pronunciation "es-q-el", and K. Date — "sequel". In a video advertising Microsoft SQL Server, Bill Gates uses the "sequel" option at the 16th second, and "S.K.L." at the 38th. The official pronunciation of the MySQL database, according to the documentation, is "May—es-q-el", PostgreSQL is "Post-gres—q-el", while the developers of Microsoft SQL Server and Oracle prefer the pronunciation of "sequel", for example, in terms such as PL/SQL and T-SQL.
